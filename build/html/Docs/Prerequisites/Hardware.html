Hardware¶

Having a good swerve starts with having good hardware. Here is a short summary of potential swerve hardware you might use.

Modules:¶

Swerve Drive Specialties:¶

  • MK4 Module

    • Cost (not including motors): $300-310 USD

    • Weight (with motors): 2.2 kg (Neo), 2.4 kg (Falcon)

    • Notable Features: Very robust and used widely among FRC teams

  • MK4i Module

    • Cost (not including motors): $365 USD

    • Weight (including motors): 2.7 kg (Neo), 2.9 kg (Falcon)

    • Notable Features: Compact, robust, and popular among FRC teams

West Coast Products:¶

  • Swerve X

    • Cost (not including motors): $305-325 USD

    • Weight (not including motors): 1.2-1.6 kg (depends on the model)

    • Notable Features: Very configurable, many different options to choose from

REV Robotics:¶

  • MAXSwerve

    • Cost (not including motors): $300 USD

    • Weight (including motors): 1.7 kg (Neo), 1.8 kg (Falcon)

    • Notable Features: Incredibly small and lightweight

Homemade Swerve:¶

  • Your Imagination

    • Cost (not including motors): time and money

    • Weight (not including motors): \([0, \infty]\)

    • Notable Features: You can customize it any way you want

Sensor Customization¶

FRC teams often use the CTRE CANCoder for measuring the angle of the rotor. It is a great sensor but it can be a bit expensive. Thus, teams with a tighter budget can consider using the Thrifty Absolute Magnetic Encoder. This encoder uses the same mount as normal CANCoders, but come at a much cheaper price.

Conclusion¶

As shown above, there are numerous ways you can create your custom swerve, all of which have their advantages and disadvantages. In the end, what really matters is if your team has enough time and money to make it happen.
